Week 5: Evaluation

This week, we will introduce the most common ways that UX designers evaluate the effectiveness and usability of designs, including think-aloud usability tests. We will explore how to conduct these evaluations, including preparation before, activities during, and what to do with the results. You will learn how to conduct a usability evaluation of a design and gain an understanding of what to do with the results in order to make recommendations and improvements.

It should be clear that this week we are spending time in the “evaluate” portion of the design process!

By the end of this week, you will:

  • Gain experience executing a usability test on your prototype
  • Gain familiarity with how to report findings in a test report
  • Increase your understanding of how to make recommendations research findings

  1. 1. Watch"Watching Users"

    The focus of this week’s content is to gain familiarity with the tools and methods used to understand what users are doing and why they are doing it. Many times, we will want to combine methods, such as Google Analytics and usability testing, in order to get a richer picture of what is going on when people interact with our designs. Usability testing is a great way to get feedback on wireframes and prototypes and can be conducted within any budget while still yielding meaningful results.

  2. 2. Readin Universal Methods of Design

    To get prepared for this week’s assignment, you’ll want to read more details on how to conduct a think-aloud usability test and how to prepare a report of your findings.

    Read:

    • A/B testing (pg 8-9)
    • Think-aloud protocol (pg 180-181)
    • Usability reports (pg 192-193)

    Jakob Nielsen’s research concludes that testing with zero users will provide zero results (obviously), but even testing with one user can be useful. However, as a general rule, he recommends testing with 3-5 users per design iteration, in order to get the maximum returns on your efforts.

    Read more about the reasoning behind “Why you only need to test with 5 users”

  3. 3. Readin Universal Principles of Design

    When conducting a usability test or other design-based research, it is important to understand that while the research is not considered scientific, you do want your results to be as clean as possible. This means that you need to be aware of "priming" your users before and during an evaluation session and otherwise exposing them to stimuli that would affect their responses.

    Read:

    • Priming (pg 186-187)
    • Exposure effect (pg 86-87)
