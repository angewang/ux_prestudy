Week 5: Evaluation

This week, we will introduce the most common ways that UX designers evaluate the effectiveness and usability of designs, including think-aloud usability tests. We will explore how to conduct these evaluations, including preparation before, activities during, and what to do with the results. You will learn how to conduct a usability evaluation of a design and gain an understanding of what to do with the results in order to make recommendations and improvements.

It should be clear that this week we are spending time in the “evaluate” portion of the design process!

By the end of this week, you will:

  • Gain experience executing a usability test on your prototype
  • Gain familiarity with how to report findings in a test report
  • Increase your understanding of how to make recommendations research findings

  1. WatchGymnasium UX Fundamentals – Lesson 6: Watching Users (33 min)

    The focus of this week’s content is to gain familiarity with the tools and methods used to understand what users are doing and why they are doing it. Many times, we will want to combine methods, such as Google Analytics and usability testing, in order to get a richer picture of what is going on when people interact with our designs. Usability testing is one of the simplest ways to get feedback on wireframes and prototypes and can be conducted within any budget while still yielding meaningful results.

    Go to Gymnasium Lesson 6: Watching users

  2. Readin Universal Methods of Design

    To get prepared for this week’s assignment, you’ll want to read more details on how to conduct a think-aloud protocol, and how to prepare an evaluation report to share with the business team.

    Read:

    • A/B testing (pg 8-9)
    • Think-aloud protocol (pg 180-181)
    • Usability reports (pg 192-193)

    Jakob Nielsen’s research concludes that testing with zero users will provide zero results (obviously), but even testing with one user can be useful. However, as a general rule, he recommends testing with 3-5 users per design iteration, in order to get the maximum returns on your efforts.

    Read more about the reasoning behind “Why you only need to test with 5 users”

  3. Readin Universal Principles of Design

    When conducting a usability test (or design evaluation of any kind, really), it is important to understand that while the research is not considered scientific, you do want your results to be as clean as possible. This means that you need to be aware of "priming" your users before and during an evaluation session and otherwise exposing them to stimuli that would affect their responses.

    Read:

    • Priming (pg 186-187)
    • Exposure effect (pg 86-87)

  7. Completea usability test of your prototype & prepare a brief report by Sunday, 11:59pm

    For many of you, this assignment may be the first usability test that you are conducting in a formal manner. If you’re uncomfortable or unsure of yourself, don’t worry—we will continue conducting tests of this nature a number of times throughout the program, and you’ll continually improve with practice. However, the best way to keep yourself as calm and prepared as possible is to prepare a test script. This is something even seasoned experts do, as shown in this presentation and this formal report sample.

    Assignment directions:

    1. Download and review the sample test script from Krug’s website
    2. Edit the test script to make sense for your test goals and prototype. Print a copy for yourself to use during the test sessions.
    3. Identify 3-5 people from your target audience that you can use as participants.
    4. Conduct your test at least 3 times, taking notes about what you’re learning and wondering.
    5. When you’re done, use Illustrator to prepare a 3-page presentation. This should include what you tested, what you learned, and what you recommend for changes. Support your recommendations with results from testing.
    6. Export your file as a PDF and upload to Dropbox.
    7. Submit your URL to the assigment portal.

  8. Completethe Strengthsfinder assessment and review your results

    We'll email you an access code and a link to take a Strengthsfinder quiz this week.

    Strengthsfinder is an assessment to help you identify which abilities you lead with as your personal strengths. The quiz generates a personalized insights report and an Action-Planning Guide. Completing the quiz and reading the results should take about 30-45 minutes.

    We're introducing you to Strenghtsfinder because:

    • It's a good way to self-reflect on your own work style, learn about your fellow students, and understand how others prefer to tackle challenges within group environments.
    • It's an assessment widely used within hiring partner organizations, so being able to speak confidently to your Strengths results may help you within future job interviews.
