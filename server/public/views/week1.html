Week 1: UX Fundamentals

This week, we will start to explore some of the common principles, tools, and methods that are essential for being an effective UXer. You will start creating UX artifacts through hand sketching. You will also gain experience with conducting directed storytelling, a key UX design research method, in order to help you identify the goals of your users. Most of this week’s content will keep us focused on the “Identify” portion of our design process. This stage helps us know what we’re designing and why, and is essential to working effectively in subsequent stages in the design process.

Our goals for this week are for you to:

  • Understand the UX process model and the principles of user-centered design
  • Know the importance of personas and their role in the UX design process
  • Gain experience with converting ideas into hand drawn sketches
  • Gain experience with conducting directed storytelling

  2. 2. Readfrom Universal Principles of Design and Universal Methods of Design

    The following methods introduced this week are common to use when doing discovery research at the start of a design project. You will be conducting directed storytelling, so be sure to read about that method especially carefully!

    Contextual research is an ethnographic research method that puts you into your user’s context in order to better understand how they do their work and achieve their goals.

    Continue reading about contextual inquiry (Universal Methods, pg. 58-59)

    Directed storytelling is a way to elicit meaningful stories from participants about their previous experiences with products or services.

    Continue reading about directed storytelling (Universal Methods, pg. 90-91)

    Personas are a fictional representation of your users, based on synthesized research findings. They are typically used to guide the design team’s decisions and keep everyone focused on user goals.

    Continue reading about personas (Universal Methods, pg. 170-171; Universal Principles, pg 182-183)

    (These readings are from Universal Methods of Design and Universal Principles of Design. Digital versions are available in the coursework channel in Slack; physical versions will be included in your Welcome Kit.)

  3. 3. Readabout sketching and critique

    Sketching is an essential skill for a designer to have, which is why we’ll be working on it through the duration of the program. If you feel concerned about your sketching skills, don’t worry. Many people equate sketching with “being good at drawing” but that couldn’t be farther from the truth. As a designer, pen & paper are the cheapest and most accessible tools used to communicate ideas or translate words into visuals that can be understood by everyone. Sometimes, it’s all you need to get everyone on the same page and keep the team moving forward!

    One fun way to get more comfortable with sketches is to start taking “sketchnotes” of the week’s lecture materials! You’ll be able to practice some of the principles we are learning about (e.g. hierarchy, chunking), as well as practice thinking on the fly while letting go of perfectionism! A double-win!

    Critique is another activity that designers are used to, and might seem unusual or scary when you first start. But once you experience the power of an effective critique, you’ll might wonder how you could ever design without one! Critiquing is a professional forum used for giving and receiving feedback on how well your designs are achieving their goals. It is not a criticism of your personal abilities, but rather a professional discussion about your work. Critique is a great place to exercise and strengthen your design vocabulary while practicing your presentation and listening skills.

    Read more about how Design Critiques: Encourage a Positive Culture to Improve Products.

  6. 6. Complete & SUBMITdirected storytelling with 3 participants & a brief report by Sunday, 9:00pm

    It is easy as humans to make assumptions about what people want. As User Experience professionals, we are always working to confirm or deny assumptions by finding out what users really need. Throughout the program, we’ll work through a number of methods that UXers employ to get this information.

    One method is to conduct directed storytelling with your users, in order to listen to and uncover insights from their firsthand experiences. Now is a great time to practice this research method, as you’ll be required to do more of it once you get into the classroom. If you need to refresh your understanding of the method you can refer back to the reading assigned earlier this week. The main objective is to learn about a memorable experience your users had in order to use those insights to help you design a more meaningful experience in the future.

Take it further

  1. ReadChapter 1 in The Design of Everyday Things by Don Norman

    This book is a seminal piece on the psychology of usability and user experience, using relatable examples from everyday life to explain the theories. Many practitioners have read this book cover to cover a number of times; usually, this book can be the “lightbulb moment” for those realizing that they’ve been thinking like a UXer without even knowing what that was or meant!

    To enhance your understanding of the psychological principles behind what we’re discovering about users and designs in Tier 1, read chapters 1-2. Keep going if you’re feeling inspired!
