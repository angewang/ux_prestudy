Week 1: UX Fundamentals

This week, we will start to explore some of the common principles, tools, and methods that are essential to designing effectively. You will start your journey of creating design artifacts, through hand sketching. You will also gain experience with conducting contextual inquiry, a key UX design research method, in order to help you identify the goals of your users. Most of this week’s content will keep us focused on the “Identify” portion of our design process, although we will dip a bit into prototyping for early practice. This stage helps us know what we’re designing and why, and is essential to working effectively in subsequent stages!

Our goals for this week are for you to:

  • Understand the UX process model and the principles of user-centered design
  • Know the importance of personas and their role in the UX design process
  • Gain experience with converting ideas into hand drawn sketches
  • Gain familiarity with using digital tools (Adobe Illustrator)
  • Gain experience with conducting contextual inquiry

  2. Read from Universal Principles of Design and Universal Methods of Design

    Two key concepts that are being introduced this week are that of personas and contextual inquiry. Contextual research is an ethnographic research method that puts you into your user’s context in order to better understand how they do their work and achieve their goals (as pertains to the design you’re working on). Personas are a fictional representation of your users, (ideally) based on the synthesis of the research you’ve done with users in your target segments. They are typically used to guide the design team’s decisions and keep everyone focused on user goals.

    Read more about Contextual inquiry( pdf) and Personas (pdf).

    (These readings are from Universal Methods of Design and Universal Principles of Design. Both books will be provided to you during your on-site visit next week. PDFs for future readings will not be provided.)

  3. Readabout Sketching and Critique

    Sketching is an essential skill for a designer to have, which is why we’ll be working on it through the duration of the program. (It’s also the main reason why you’re required to invest in some good pens and a sketchbook!) If you feel concerned about your sketching skills, don’t worry. Many people equate sketching with “being good at drawing” but that couldn’t be farther from the truth. As a designer, pen & paper is the cheapest and most accessible tool used to communicate ideas or translate words into visuals that can be understood by everyone. Sometimes, it’s all you need to get everyone on the same page and keep the team moving forward!

    One fun way to get more comfortable with sketches is to start taking “sketchnotes” of the week’s lecture materials! You’ll be able to practice some of the principles we are learning about (e.g. hierarchy, chunking), as well as practice thinking on the fly while letting go of perfectionism! A double-win!

    Critique is another activity that designers are used to, and might seem unusual or scary when you first start. But once you experience the power of an effective critique, you’ll might wonder how you could ever design without them! Critiquing is a professional forum used for giving and receiving feedback on how well (or not) your designs are achieving their goals. It is not a discussion of your personal abilities, but rather a professional discussion about your work. Critique a great place to exercise and strengthen your design vocabulary while practicing your presentation and listening skills. Read more about how Design Critiques: Encourage a Positive Culture to Improve Products.

  8. Completea user research assignment

    Although you were thinking about a particular audience when you made your TV remote sketches, you got away with making a lot of assumptions about what that group of people wants from that device. UX designers are always working to confirm or deny assumptions by finding out what users really need, directly from the source (ideally). Throughout the program, we’ll work through a number of ways that designers work to identify and define user goals.

    One way research user needs that is to go “in-situ” or into the context of your users in order to find out how they work and play, what makes them frustrated or delighted, and where the opportunities are for your design to improve their lives. This is called contextual inquiry, as you know from your earlier reading.

    Now is a great time to start to get comfortable with this research method, as you’ll be required to do more of it once you get into the classroom. If you need to refresh your understanding of the method you can refer back to the reading assigned earlier this week.

    NOTE: This assignment will continue for the rest of Prework, so choose your application wisely!

    Directions:

    • Choose one of two tasks: (1) finding and booking a commercial airline flight or (2) finding a restaurant and booking a meal reservation. Decide whether you want to design a website or smartphone app for the chosen task.
    • Define an audience by completing the statement: “My user is a _____, who wants to [find and book a flight OR find a restaurant and book a meal reservation].” For example: My user is a working professional who wants to find and book a flight, or my user is a retiree who wants to find a restaurant and book a meal reservation. Make sure that you choose a group of people that you can easily meet with, as you’ll be required to do research with them twice throughout Prework.
    • Pick three people within your audience and have them complete the task you’ve chosen (in step 1) using an existing tool of the same medium. For example: If you have chosen to do a flight booking website, you could watch them use Kayak.com; if you have chosen to design a restaurant reservation app, you could watch people use the OpenTable app.
    • Type up your findings, identifying commonalities and/or differences amongst your three research participants. Include a revised goals statement that shows the results of your research, for example: My user is a working professional who wants to quickly find a trendy place for happy hour and date nights, or my user is a retiree who wants to find an economical and safe place to vacation with his spouse.
    • Submit your findings as a PDF to the Assignments portal page.

  1. Read from Universal Principles of Design

    Before you embark on creating your first set of wireframes, it’s critical that you understand two key principles: hierarchy and mental models. Hierarchy is a simple principle that can be difficult to execute: it means that the elements in your design clearly communicate various levels of importance. In other words, users can, at a glance, discern what’s most important, less important, and what is included as supporting details. A mental model is a user’s understanding of how a design should work. Users employ their mental models in order to make sense of and navigate a world that frequently has them interacting with new products, interfaces, and experiences. Major catastrophes can happen when a user’s mental model is misunderstood or not considered at all in a new or re-design.

    Read about Hierarchy (PDF) (pg. 124-125) and Mental models (PDF) (pg 154-155) in Universal Principles of Design.

  2. Watchexplanations and demonstrations of a “conceptual model”

    Conceptual models can be one of the toughest ideas to explain and to understand. We introduce it this week through a variety of videos and readings that provide definitions and examples. However, it might still not make sense – and that’s ok. We’ll continue to discuss and demonstrate what a conceptual model (also called “mental model”) is throughout the entire program. Eventually, it will start to make sense and you’ll even be able to explain it to other people!
