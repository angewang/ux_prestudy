  2. Read from Universal Principles of Design and Universal Methods of Design

    Two key concepts that are being introduced this week are that of personas and contextual inquiry. Contextual research is an ethnographic research method that puts you into your user’s context in order to better understand how they do their work and achieve their goals (as pertains to the design you’re working on). Personas are a fictional representation of your users, (ideally) based on the sy nthesis of the research you’ve done with users in your target segments. They are typically used to guide the design team’s decisions and keep everyone focused on user goals.

  3. Readabout Sketching and Critique

    Sketching is an essential skill for a designer to have, which is why we’ll be working on it through the duration of the program. (It’s also the main reason why you’re required to invest in some good pens and a sketchbook!) If you feel concerned about your sketching skills, don’t worry. Many people equate sketching with “being good at drawing” but that couldn’t be farther from the truth. As a designer, pen & paper is the cheapest and most accessible tool used to communicate ideas or translate words into visuals that can be understood by everyone. Sometimes, it’s all you need to get everyone on the same page and keep the team moving forward!

    One fun way to get more comfortable with sketches is to start taking “sketchnotes” of the week’s lecture materials! You’ll be able to practice some of the principles we are learning about (e.g. hierarchy, chunking), as well as practice thinking on the fly while letting go of perfectionism! A double-win!

    Critique is another activity that designers are used to, and might seem unusual or scary when you first start. But once you experience the power of an effective critique, you’ll might wonder how you could ever design without them! Critiquing is a professional forum used for giving and receiving feedback on how well (or not) your designs are achieving their goals. It is not a discussion of your personal abilities, but rather a professional discussion about your work. Critique a great place to exercise and strengthen your design vocabulary while practicing your presentation and listening skills. Read more about how Design Critiques: Encourage a Positive Culture to Improve Products.

    Although you were thinking about a particular audience when you made your TV remote sketches, you got away with making a lot of assumptions about what that group of people wants from that device. UX designers are always working to confirm or deny assumptions by finding out what users really need, directly from the source (ideally). Throughout the program, we’ll work through a number of ways that designers work to identify and define user goals.

    One way research user needs that is to go “in-situ” or into the context of your users in order to find out how they work and play, what makes them frustrated or delighted, and where the opportunities are for your design to improve their lives. This is called contextual inquiry, as you know from your earlier reading.

    Now is a great time to start to get comfortable with this research method, as you’ll be required to do more of it once you get into the classroom. If you need to refresh your understanding of the method you can refer back to the reading assigned earlier this week.

  1. Read from Universal Principles of Design

    Before you embark on creating your first set of wireframes, it’s critical that you understand two key principles: hierarchy and mental models. Hierarchy is a simple principle that can be difficult to execute: it means that the elements in your design clearly communicate various levels of importance. In other words, users can, at a glance, discern what’s most important, less important, and what is included as supporting details. A mental model is a user’s understanding of how a design should work. Users employ their mental models in order to make sense of and navigate a world that frequently has them interacting with new products, interfaces, and experiences. Major catastrophes can happen when a user’s mental model is misunderstood or not considered at all in a new or re-design.

    Read about Hierarchy (PDF) (pg. 124-125) and Mental models (PDF) (pg 154-155) in Universal Principles of Design.

    Now that we’re getting into the most complicated and meaningful pieces of a design, you may find yourself wondering “How will I ever be able to design all of this well?” It’s ok to feel this way, as it means that you are recognizing the importance of every single component of a product or experience, beyond the visual styling of that artifact (which is what many people think of when they hear the word “design”).   1. WatchGymnasium UX Fundamentals – Lesson 5: Best Practices

    When you are just learning how to design effective interfaces, it can be useful to leverage existing UI patterns and well-established conventions. Sometimes, there is reason to create a new convention (as when the old is a mental model mismatch) and other times we need to break convention to best meet our goals. But as a new designer, this is less frequently the case. Interestingly, the more you become familiar with conventions, you’ll find that there is a dark side to them; most people refer to these as “dark patterns.” Dark patterns toe a fine line between matching user expectations and manipulating expectations, usually for business gains. We’ll learn more about these and discuss their implications in Slack!

  2. Readfrom Universal Principles of Design

    One of the reasons we encourage you to start your designs with UI elements that are common and consistent with user expectations is because it saves your users time when they are trying to figure out how to use your new website or app. All UI elements afford some sort of interaction, and some afford many ways of interacting. Limiting these options to those most common can help you be more effective when first starting out as a designer. The more evaluations you conduct and learn about your users, the more likely it is that you can innovate or at least improve upon the elements you are using.

This week, we will introduce the most common ways that UX designers evaluate the effectiveness and usability of designs, including think-aloud usability tests. We will explore how to conduct these evaluations, including preparation before, activities during, and what to do with the results. You will learn how to conduct a usability evaluation of a design and gain an understanding of what to do with the results of a test session (or group of sessions) in order to improve upon a design. By the end of this week, you should be able to:

    Read about the methods of A/B testing, (pg 8-9) Think-aloud protocol (pg 180-181) and Usability reports (pg 192-193) in Universal Methods of Design.

    Jakob Nielsen’s research concludes that testing with zero users will provide zero results (obviously), but even testing with one user can be useful. However, as a general rule, he recommends testing with 3-5 users per design iteration, in order to get the maximum returns on your efforts.

  3. Readin Universal Principles of Design

    When conducting a usability test (or design evaluation of any kind, really), it is important to understand that while the research is not considered scientific, you do want your results to be as clean as possible. This means that you need to be aware of priming (or conditioning) your users before and during an evaluation session and otherwise exposing them to stimuli that would affect their responses.

    Read about the principles of Priming (pg 186-187) and Exposure effect (pg 86-87) in Universal Principles of Design.

  1. Readabout good practices for UX portfolios

    As a junior UX designer, it’s essential to have a portfolio that showcases and demonstrates your ability to succinctly, but meaningfully talk about your work, your process, and the reasons for your design decisions (i.e. your “design rationale”). These things are often shown along side samples of your work (wireframes, prototypes, affinity diagrams, journey maps, etc), but are as important (if not more important than) the design artifacts themselves. The presentation of your portfolio should be visually appealing with a clear hierarchy, with work samples shown at various stages of fidelity, such as seen in Brienne Moore’s portfolio.

    Michael Nguyen is another great example of a UX designer recently entering the field from an architecture background. His case studies are highly narrative and introspective, and he does a great job breaking down how he thought through the design process on each project.

    By the time you’re a senior UX designer, you will likely have worked with large clients and have signed NDAs that precludes you from showing samples of your actual work. However, this doesn’t excuse you from having a portfolio, as this example of Kate Caldwell’s portfolio demonstrates. She still tells a story, discusses her role in, and provides a client perspective for each project.
