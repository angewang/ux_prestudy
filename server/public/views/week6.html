Week 6: Portfolio documentation

This last week, we will cover the basics of providing compelling design rationale in various ways. We will reflect on the previously learned content, and practice documenting what was done, why it was done, and what was designed as a result. The focus will be on creating documentation that effectively communicates design rationale and outcomes throughout the design process.

  1. Readabout good practices for UX portfolios

    As a junior UX designer, it’s essential to have a portfolio that showcases and demonstrates your ability to succinctly, but meaningfully talk about your work, your process, and the reasons for your design decisions (i.e. your “design rationale”). These things are often shown along side samples of your work (wireframes, prototypes, affinity diagrams, journey maps, etc), but are as important (if not more important than) the design artifacts themselves. The presentation of your portfolio should be visually appealing with a clear hierarchy, with work samples shown at various stages of fidelity.

    Christina Richardson has recently found her fit in UX, after a career path that includes related fields such as psychology, research, and front-end development. Her featured case studies are similar to the work you will be doing at Prime.

    Michael Nguyen is a great example of a UXer recently entering the field from an architecture background. His case studies are highly narrative and introspective, and he does a great job breaking down how he thought through the design process on each project.

    By the time you’re a senior UXer, you will likely have worked with large clients and have signed NDAs that precludes you from showing samples of your actual work. However, this doesn’t excuse you from having a portfolio, as this example of Kate Caldwell’s portfolio demonstrates. She still tells a story, discusses her role in, and provides a client perspective for each project.

  2. Startyour UX portfolio

    You’ll be working on adding projects to your portfolio and adjusting the format throughout the duration of the program. This week, we want to make sure you have a URL and that your web hosting is properly setup.

    Two recommended services are Squarespace and Wordpress. Both services manage the web hosting for you and allow you to customize the code on your site, something you’ll want to do once you feel comfortable with HTML & CSS. Wordpress offers a free service and both offer low-cost monthly fee option.
