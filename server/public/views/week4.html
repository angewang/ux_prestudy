Week 4: UI patterns & Interactive prototypes

This week, we will go further into understanding how designers can more effectively match user expectations using well-established user-interface (UI) design patterns. You will start to understand how and when to employ best practices and explore when it may be appropriate to break convention. We’ll also be introducing you to HTML & CSS, two common languages used in the design of websites.

By the end of this week, you will:

  • Understand how digital design elements can match or conflict with user expectations
  • Transform digital wireframes into an interactive prototype using Axure
  • Begin to understand the basics of HTML & CSS

Coursework

  1. 1. Watch"Best Practices"

    When you are just learning how to design effective interfaces, it can be useful to leverage existing UI patterns and well-established conventions. Sometimes, there is reason to create a new convention (as when the old is a mental model mismatch) and other times we need to break convention to best meet our goals. But as a new designer, this is less frequently the case. Interestingly, the more you become familiar with conventions, you’ll find that there is a dark side to them; most people refer to these as “dark patterns.” Dark patterns toe a fine line between matching user expectations and manipulating expectations, usually for business gains. We’ll learn more about these and discuss their implications in Slack!

    Go to Gymnasium Lesson 5: Best Practices (Skip the "Lesson Conclusion")

  2. 2. Readfrom Universal Principles of Design

    One of the reasons we encourage you to start your designs with UI elements that are common and consistent with user expectations is because it saves your users time when they are trying to figure out how to use your new website or app. All UI elements afford some sort of interaction, and some afford many ways of interacting. Limiting these options to those most common can help you be more effective when first starting out as a designer. The more evaluations you conduct and learn about your users, the more likely it is that you can innovate or improve upon the elements you are using.

    Read about:

    • Affordance (pg 22-23)
    • Consistency (pg 56-57)

Practical Teamwork: Growth Mindset

  1. Watch Maintaining a growth mindset

    Growth Mindset is a concept from psychological research on human motivation and learning. It’s the outlook that academic, personal, and professional growth comes from consistent effort.

    A growth mindset counters the cultural myth that we hear so frequently: that being good at something means that it comes easily to us (i.e., it’s low-effort). Research shows that our internalized beliefs about what it means to be “smart” has a significant affect on our long-term goals and learning outcomes.

    Takeaways:
    • Effort activates ability.
    • Setbacks and mistakes are a natural part of learning. It’s what happens when we take on valuable challenges.
    • Recognize your effort and partial-wins when you engage in challenging work! Praise the process of diving into tough problems.
