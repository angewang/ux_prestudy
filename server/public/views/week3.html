Week 3: Content Strategy & Information Architecture

This week, we will focus on the meaning of content within our designs, giving more attention to the page/screen flow and navigation language. We will discuss methods for improving usability by creating content that is more easily understood by our users. We will also cover ways to evaluate how well our content is working for our users. You will expand on your work from previous weeks by adding a page/screen flow and adding the screens necessary for your user to complete their primary tasks. For this reason, we’ll remain in the “prototyping” phase of our design process.

Our goals for this week are for you to:

  • Understand the purpose and use of ‘best practices’ or convention within design solutions
  • Gain familiarity with methods and tools used to understand content structuring
  • Gain experience in creating a meaningful and effective interaction model for a website/app, based on previously conducted user research

  3. 3. Readfrom Universal Principles of Design

    Now that we’re getting into the most complicated and meaningful pieces of a design, you may find yourself wondering “How will I ever be able to design all of this well?” It’s ok to feel this way, as it means that you are recognizing the importance of every single component of a product or experience, beyond the visual styling of that artifact. While the visuals are incredibly critical, they are not the only layer of information that needs consideration. If the meaning, organization, and process of interacting with each component is not carefully considered and evaluated, the visual styling will simply be a nice looking layer on a poorly crafted experience.

    We will spend a lot of time throughout this program talking about and implementing the principles that support good design. This week, we’ll focus on a couple organizational principles that help you achieve strong hierarchy: chunking and five hat racks.

    Read about:

    • Chunking (pg 40-41)
    • Five hat racks (pg 100-101)
  4. 4. Readexcerpt from Don’t Make Me Think by Steve Krug

    Steve Krug has long been a prophet of usability principles and spreads his message through engaging books, videos, and other content. Many people find his books approachable and invaluable, even after being in publication for well over a decade. Much of what he writes about is a practical application and evaluation of the principles we will be discussing throughout the course.

    Take a few minutes to read the brief four-page excerpt from Don’t Make Me Think: A Common Sense Approach to Web Usability, available for free on Krug’s website.
